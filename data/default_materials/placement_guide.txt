CHAPTER 1: BIG TECH HIRING PROCESS & EXPECTATIONS

1.1 Google Software Engineering (SWE) Placements
Google's technical recruitment relies heavily on data structures and algorithms (DSA). A standard interview process includes:
- Online Assessment (OA): 2 questions in 90 minutes. Focused on graph algorithms, dynamic programming, or advanced string manipulation.
- Technical Rounds (3-4 rounds): 45 minutes each. Candidates are expected to talk through their thought process, write clean code, analyze time and space complexity, and optimize the solution. Topics: trees, graphs (Dijkstra's, DFS, BFS), heap, binary search, and sliding window.
- Googleyness & Leadership (1 round): Behavioral round evaluating cultural alignment, feedback handling, ambiguity management, and bias-free execution.
Tip: Always dry run your code with edge cases (e.g., empty array, extremely large values, duplicates).

1.2 Microsoft Software Engineering Internships
Microsoft values software craftsmanship and system design fundamentals alongside coding skills.
- Codility/Online Test: 3 coding questions covering arrays, hashing, and sorting.
- Technical Round 1 (Algorithms): Binary search, linked list operations, tree traversals, and recursion.
- Technical Round 2 (System Design/OOD): Focus on Object-Oriented Design (e.g., design a parking lot, design an elevator system, or design a reservation system).
- Director Round: Technical-behavioral hybrid. Includes deep dives into projects, low-level design, database normalization, and behavioral questions like "Why Microsoft?".
Tip: Make sure you understand OOP design principles (SOLID principles) and clean coding practices.

1.3 Amazon Software Engineering Recruitment
Amazon's hiring process is unique due to its strong alignment with its 16 Leadership Principles (LPs).
- Online Assessment: Coding challenges, work style assessment, and a simulated system debugging module.
- Technical Rounds (3 rounds): Each round starts with 15-20 minutes of Behavioral questions testing specific LPs (e.g., Customer Obsession, Ownership, Dive Deep, Deliver Results). The remaining 30 minutes focus on coding or system design.
- Bar Raiser Round: Conducted by an independent interviewer to ensure the candidate raises the median capability level of the company. Heavily focuses on scalability, databases, and trade-off analysis.
Tip: When answering Amazon behavioral questions, structure your answers using the STAR method (Situation, Task, Action, Result) and explicitly map them to Leadership Principles.

CHAPTER 2: TECHNICAL CORE CONCEPTS PREPARATION

2.1 Data Structures & Algorithms (DSA) Roadmap
- Arrays & Hashing: Master sliding window, two-pointer techniques, and prefix sums. Understand hash maps and hash sets.
- Trees & Graphs: Know DFS, BFS, Union-Find, Dijkstra's algorithm, and topological sorting.
- Dynamic Programming: Learn memoization (top-down) and tabulation (bottom-up). Practice classical problems like 0/1 Knapsack, Longest Common Subsequence (LCS), and Edit Distance.
- Heaps & Priority Queues: Essential for top-K elements problems.

2.2 System Design & Scalability
- Microservices: Understand API Gateways, Service Discovery, and Load Balancing.
- Caching: Learn Redis/Memcached. Know caching strategies (write-through, write-back, cache-aside) and eviction policies (LRU, LFU).
- Databases: Understand SQL (PostgreSQL, MySQL) vs. NoSQL (MongoDB, Cassandra). Master horizontal partitioning (sharding), replication models (master-slave), and ACID properties.
- Message Queues: Understand asynchronous processing using RabbitMQ, Celery, or Apache Kafka.

CHAPTER 3: BEHAVIORAL INTERVIEWS & THE STAR METHOD

The STAR method is the gold standard for behavioral questions:
- Situation: Describe the context, project, or problem you faced. Keep it concise.
- Task: Define what goal or responsibility you had to manage.
- Action: Outline the exact steps YOU took. Explain why you made those decisions. Use "I", not "We".
- Result: Present the quantitative and qualitative outcomes of your actions (e.g., "reduced query latency by 35%", "delivered the product 2 days ahead of schedule").

Common Behavioral Questions to prepare:
1. "Tell me about a time you had to deliver a project under a tight deadline."
2. "Describe a scenario where you disagreed with your manager or team lead. How did you handle it?"
3. "Give an example of a project that failed. What did you learn?"
4. "Describe how you motivated a team member who was underperforming."
